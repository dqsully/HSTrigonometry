Point-Slope Form for a Line

Point-Slope Form for a Line

The equation for a line can come in many forms, and, if you want to be lazy, you can represent it in Point-Slope Form. Point-Slope Form is basically the median equation of Slope and Slope-Intercept Form. The actual form is `y-y_1=m(x-x_1)`. This represents the fact that the difference in two points' y values is proportional to the diference in their x values by a multiplier m. This form could easily be converted into the equation to find slope by dividing both sides by `x-x_1`, making it `m=(y-y_1)/(x-x_1)`, or it can be converted to Slope Intercept form by distributing `m` and adding `y` to both sides.

For an example, let's say we were given the slope: `3/2` and a point on the line: `(6,5)` and have to find the Slope-Intercept Form of that line:
First, we fill in the information we already know: `y-5=(3/2)(x-6)`
Then, we distribute `m` or the slope: `y-5=(3/2)x-9`
And add `y_1` to both sides: `y-5+5=(3/2)x-9+5`
And out comes our answer: `y=(3/2)x-4`
Now let's say that we have two points on a line: `(7,4)` and `(3,3)` and had to find the slope of that line:
First, we fill in the information we already know: `4-3=m(7-3)`
Then, we divide both sides by `(x-x_1)` to get the Slope Equation: `m(7-3)/(7-3)=(4-3)/(7-3)`
Simplify it: `m=(4-3)/(7-3)`
And out comes our answer: `m=1/4`

Where would this be used? This sort of math pops up in programs everywhere, including your Operating System (Windows/Mac OSX/Linux), your browser, and pretty much every single video game you have ever played. To use video games as an example, a 2D platformer with a ray-gun would need to calculate the slope of the line between your avatar and your cursor to show the beam. But, let's say you're not interested in computers. Well, if you do any sort of construction you pretty much need to use this, and if do any sort of statistics, it will certainly pop up.

Laws of Exponents and Simplifying Expressions with Exponents

Laws of Exponents and Simplifying Expressions with Exponents

There is a set of laws that apply to exponents and make it possible for us to solve expressions that have exponents. The laws are as follows, so don't let the number of `x`'s and `y`'s overwhelm you:

Law Example Explanation
`x^y` where `y>0` is equal to `1` multiplied to `x` `y` times `5^3=(1)*5*5*5` This is the basic purpose of exponents, to add another level to multiplication the same way multiplication did to addition.
`x^0=1` `1000^0=1` When there is nothing to multiply `1` to, `1` simply remains. Or, `1/1=1` and `x/x=1`.
`x^y` where `y<0` is equal to `1` divided by `x` `y` times `5^-3=1/(5*5*5)` This is still a purpose of exponents, just applied to negative numbers.
With the three above laws, when the exponent increases by 1, the value multiplies by x, when the exponent decreases by 1, the value divides by x.
`1/x^y=x^-y` `1/x^3=x^-3` `1/((x*x*x))=(1/(x*x*x))` or `1/((1/(x*x*x))) = (x*x*x)`.
`x^(1/y)=rooty x` `x^(1/3)=root3 x` `1/x` is the inverse of x, so in an exponent it results in the inverse: a root.
`x^y*x^z=x^(y+z)` `x^3*x^1=x x x*x=x^4` This is has the same type of reason that `x*y+x*z=x*(y+z)`, that we multiply `x` `y` times, and then `x` another `z` times, for a total of `y+z` times.
`x^y/x^z=x^(y-z)` `x^3/x^4=(x x x)/(x x x x)=1/x=x^-1` This expands out to `x^y+1/x^z`, which simplifies to `x^y+x^-z` and then to `x^(y-z)`.
`(x^y)^z=x^(y*z)` `(x^2)^2=(x x)^2=x x x x=x^4` First we multiply `x` `y` times, and then that another `z` times, for a total of `y*z`.
`(xy)^z=x^z*y^z` `(xy)^4=x x x xyyyy=x^4+y^4` Quite simply, we are distributing the exponent to each component of a multiplication expression because in the end it is all multiplied back together.
`(x/y)^z=x^z/y^z` `(x/y)^4=(x x x x)/(yyyy)=x^4/y^4` The same as previously, except some components are divided, but it is at the same level of operation because multiplication and division oppose directly.
`x^(y/z)=rootz x^y` `x^(3/4)=(x^3)^(1/4)=root4 (x^3)` This results from a conjugation of previous rules, expanding out to the root of `x^y` in the base of `z`.
We can use these laws to simplify exponential expressions, so let's simplify this one: `((x^4*x^-7)/x^5)^(1/5)`
First we evaluate the expression inside the parentheses: `x^4*x^-7=x^-3=>(x^-3/x^5)^(1/5)`
And continue: `x^-3/x^5=x^-8=>(x^-8)^(1/5)`
And use the last rule to get our answer: `(x^-8)^(1/5)=root5 (1/x^8)`

So in just three steps we were able to turn a daunting expression like `((x^4*x^-7)/x^5)^(1/5)` into simply `root5 (1/x^8)`, which is about as complex as a simplified exponential expression can get, because of these exponential laws. This is why it is being taught and taught again, so that everyone remembers it, because you might need to use it again.

Composition of Functions

Composition of Functions

Composition of functions is compiling all unreferenced functions with their referenced functions, so there are no more function references in the end. For example, let's say we have two functions: `f(x)=10+3g(x)` and `g(x)=40x`. If we were to compose these two functions together, we would use `f(x)` as our base function and fill in any references within that. So, `3g(x)` would turn into `3(40x)`, making `f(x)=10+3(40x)`, or simplified: `f(x)=120x+10`.

Now, for a more complicated problem, let's say we have these six functions:
`a(x)=d(x)+4-30x`
`b(x)=2a(x)d(x)-10`
`c(x)=b(x)-x*a(x)`
`d(x)=47x`
`e(x)=c(x)-50`
`f(x)=(x-4)*e(x)`
The first thing we would do is map all of the references out creating six trees of functions:

In the example above, each red line shows where that function is referenced, meaning it cannot be the base function, and the only function without a red line is `f(x)`, meaning everything is going to be compiled into that function. If `f(x)` referenced another function other than `e(x)`, both `f(x)` and `e(x)` would be base functions, meaning you would end up with two functions instead of just one in the end. But, since we will end up with just `f(x)`, we need to look at every endpoint in the tree and compile upwards like so:

Step Result
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)->a(x)->d(x)` `d(x)=47x`
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)->a(x)` `(47x)+4-30x=>`
`a(x)=17x+4`
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)->b(x)` `2(17x+4)(47x)-10=>`
`b(x)=1598x^2+376x-10`
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)` `(1598x^2+376x-10)-x*(17x+4)=>`
`c(x)=1581x^2+372x-10`
`f(x)->e(x)` `(1581x^2+372x-10)-50=>`
`e(x)=1581x^2+372x-60`
`f(x)` `(x-4)*(1581x^2+372x-60)=>`
`f(x)=1581x^3-5952x^2-1548x+240`

Believe it or not, you use Function Composition for almost every math problem you solve or every expression you create. To solve a problem, you need to follow the order of operations, and by doing so, you are creating a virtual tree and parsing up it like above. For example, anything inside parentheses is basically a function of its own, it's just not named. When you are writing an expression however, you are using bits and pieces in your mathematical vocabulary to put it together like a puzzle piece, and each one of those pieces is a type of function. As for career uses, it is used all the time in computers, just not always mathematically. Function Composition can be compiling any set of functions, including functions with sets of computer instructions. Computer programs are written with functions, but they can be recursive, that is, call themselves multiple times, so the computer has to follow its function tree from the top down, but in essence still does the same thing. Sometimes, programmers will do this too, because when programs have unnessecary functions, the program gets a bit slower and larger, so for optimization, the programmer might do some of the computer's work for it.

Finding Inverse Functions

Finding Inverse Functions

When you graph the inverse of a function, something special happens: that function is reflected across the line `y=x`. To get the inverse of a function, you need to solve for the other, or another, variable. In the case of functions for graphing, we will also switch the names of `x` and `y`. So let's say we had the function `y=3x+4`, to get the inverse we would need to solve `x=3y+4` for `y`.

For a more complex example, how about we invert `y=7*4^3-5^x`. Table time!
Get the actual equation we are solving for: `7*4^3-5^y=x`
Simplify: `448-5^y=x`
Subtract by `x` and `-5^y` `5^y=448-x`
Use logarithms to isolate `y`, and we get our answer: `y=log_5(448-x)`

This concept can actually be quite useful when trying to test the input variables for a certain output. To use the above equation, let's say if we put in the number of members of a group we would get out a certain number of dollars, and if we wanted to get enough money to pay rent or whatever, we could invert the function and get how many members we would need. You might use this for planning how many people you can buy food for in a party, but most other examples are in fully mathematical applications, usually mathematical modeling and statistics.

Simplifying Square (and other) Roots and Rationalizing the Denominator

Simplifying Square (and other) Roots and Rationalizing the Denominator

When simplifying an expression, any roots of any base must be removed as much as possible, and the denominator of every fraction must not contain any roots. To simplify a root, the contents of that root must have a multiple that is of that root's base. To clarify, `rootx ((y^x)z)` can be simplified to `y*rootx z`. For a fraction, to remove any radicals, the entire fraction must be multiplied by a factor that will remove the radicals in the denominator, most likely making the numerator irrational in the process.

Let's simplify `5/root3(54x)`:
First, we have to simplify the radical: `54=3^3*2->5/(3root3(2x))`
Then remove the radical from the denominator: `(5root3(2x)^2)/(3root3(2x)^3)=(5root3(2x)^2)/(6x)`
And simplify: `(5root3(4x^2))/(6x)`
Factoring Quadratics and some Cubics

Factoring Quadratics and some Cubics

To quickly review, a quadratic is an expression in the form of `ax^2+bx+c`, but factoring them is really quite easy. There are two ways to do it: use the 'generic' method, or use the quadratic formula. The generic method works for simple cases, and if it doesn't work for your case you can use the quadratic formula instead. The steps for the generic method are laid out for you below, and `f(x,y)` is the common factor of `x` and `y`:

Step Example
Factor out all common factors `zax^2+zbx+zc=z*(ax^2+bx+c)`
Find which two numbers multiply to `ac` and add to `b` `st=ac and s+t=b->z*(ax^2+(s+t)x+c)`
Factor the resultant numbers in an array `z*(f(a,s)x+f(t,c))(f(a,t)x+f(s,c))`

This method is a bit confusing and hard to put into a formula, but if you have paper and do not have a calulator, this would be the easiest way to go. With the quadratic formula, you will want a calculator, especially since it uses roots and fractions. Below is an example of using the quadratic formula to solve a quadratic expression.

The Quadratic Formula: `(b+-sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a)`
Putting it into your expression: `a(x-(b+sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))(x-(b-sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))`
To complete the factoring, you will need to distribute factors of `a` to each parenthetic group intuitively so you get the best answer possible: `a=ts->(tx-t(b+sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))(sx-s(b-sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))`

For a cubic or any other higher polynomial, you will need to use the Rational Root Theorem, which explains that all rational roots must be in `+-(f(c_(x^n)))/(f(c_(x^0))` where `f(x)` returns all factors of `x`, `n` is the highest polynomial, `c` is the coefficient of its base, and `c_(x^n)!=0` and `c_(x^0)!=0`. So, if we have `3x^4-2`, all rational roots must fit in `+-(1,3)/(1,2)`. To find all of these roots, you can only repeat testing the expression to find inputs/roots that result in `0`, appending the root as a "parenthetic group" in the form of `(x-r)` where r is your root, and dividing the original expression by this group creating the next 'original' expression. Then, once you can't find any more roots, multiply your output by your last 'original' group, and finally intuitively distribute factors of `a` to each parenthetic group you created. Usually only mathematicians and statisticians actually use quadratics and cubics, but you will still need to know this to pass Unit 6, which will obviously come later on, so don't forget this!

Direct and Inverse Variations

Direct and Inverse Variations

When two variables have a relationship where both variables scale similarly, there are two ways to describe it: direct and inverse. A direct relationship between two variables would follow the equation `y=kx` where `k` is a constant, so that when `y` is multiplied by `a`, `x` scales by `a` as well. An inverse relationship however, would follow `y=k/x`, meaning that when `y` is multiplied by `a`, `x` scales by `1/a`, or in other words, scales inversely. However, a simplified expression that contains any constant or variable that is added or is an exponent cannot be a direct or inverse relationship, because both variables do not scale exactly inverse or the same. For example, neither `y=x+1`, `y=x^2`, or `y=x*z` can have a direct or inverse variation, unless `z` is a constant in the last example.

Solving Exponentials by Getting the same Base

Solving Exponentials by Getting the same Base

When all parts of an expression use the same base, you can remove the common base of the exponent, leaving the exponent itself. For example: `2^(x-1)=2^9=>x-1=9=>x=8`. This can be really useful for solving exponential expressions, but what happens if the equation was `2^(x-1)=4^8`? The easiest way to simplify these expressions is to simplify the base and then remove it, so that the bases are as same as they can get. So, to answer my own question, `4=2^(1+1)->4^8=2^(8+1)->2^(x-1)=4^8=2^9=>x-1=9=>x=8`.

Solving Exponentials using Logarithms

Solving Exponentials using Logarithms

To solve an exponential equation where it is not possible to use the previous method, there is another way: using logarithms. Now I have already partially covered this in the previous unit, but like the previous topic, is really simple, and only useful in modeling situations. First though, the equation needs to be simplified to `b^x=a` is any expression, so that we can use the formula `b^x=a=>x=log_b(a)`. To use this in an example, `(54-3k)^x=7=>x=log_(54-3k)(7)`. This means most likely you will be forced to use a calculator to finish the equation, but what if your calculator cannot handle logarithms of an arbitraty base? There is another formula you could use called the Change of Base Formula: `log_x(y)=ln(y)/ln(x)`, which would our example solveable on a calculator as `x=ln(7)/ln(54-3k)`.

The Logarithm Rules: Expanding/Condensing

The Logarithm Rules: Expanding/Condensing

Just like there are a bunch of laws for simplifying exponents, there are also laws for simplifying logarithms. These rules can be used to condense and expand logarithms.

Rule Explanation
`log_b(mn)=log_b(m)+log_b(n)` Multiplication inside a logarithm is equivalent to addition outside.
`log_b(m/n)=log_b(m)-log_b(n)` Division inside a logarithm is equivalent to subtraction outside.
`log_b(m^n)=n*log_b(m)` An exponent global to everything inside the logarithm is equivalent to a coefficient of the logarithm.
For an example, let's simplify `log_7(x^4)-log_7(8)`:
Remove the exponent (solve inside parentheses): `4log_7(x)-log_7(8)`
Condense the logarithms: `4log_7(x/8)`

Solving Logarithm Equations

Just like we used logarithms to solve exponential equations, we can use exponents to solve logarithmic equations. We can simply reverse the formula to solve for logarithms: `log_b(a)=x=>b^x=a`, so `log_x(16)=4=>x^4=16=>x=4`. Or, let's say we solve `log_5(x)-54=y/3` for `x`:

The first goal is isolating the logarithm: `log_5(x)=y/3+54`
Then we can replace it with an exponent: `5^(y/3+54)=x`
And finally simplify it using exponent rules: `x=5^y/3*5.55112*10^37`
Euler's Number and Natural Logs

Euler's Number and Natural Logs

Euler's number is also used frequently in computer science and programming, but it has an important role in mathematics as well. Euler discovered this number when trying to calculate the exponential growth of interest, but compounded an infinite number of times. His number is usually represented by the letter `e`, and can be calculated by an infinitely expanded sum, also proving that the number is irrational: `e=sum_(n=0)^oo1/(n!)=1+1/1+1/(1*2)+1/(1*2*3)+...`. Euler's number can be very useful in approximating things like population growth where compounding is individual and not pre-determined.

Because there are so many places to use Euler's number, it seems logical that we would make an easier way to represent a logarithm of base `e`, so se created the natural logarithm, written as `ln(x)`. We can then use the natural logarithm to get the inverse, or for example, estimate the time it would take for a population to grow to a certain point. The natural logarithm can be defined for any positive real number `a` as the area under the curve `y=1/x` from `x=1` to `x=a`.

Right Triangle Trigonometry

Right Triangle Trigonometry

I'm sure you already know this, and probably remember "soh cah toa," but if you don't, here it is again. The phrase "soh cah toa" refers to the relationships of the three basic trigonometric functions to sides of a right triangle. "soh": `sin(theta)=(sf"opposite")/(sf"hypotenuse")`, "cah": `cos(theta)=(sf"adjacent")/(sf"hypotenuse")`, and "toa": `tan(theta)=(sf"opposite")/(sf"adjacent")`. In these expressions, Theta is an angle on a right triangle that does not measure to `90^@`, the hypotenuse is the hypotenuse of that triangle, adjacent is the leg of the triangle that touches the angle, and opposite is the leg of the triangle that does not.

With each trigonometric function, there is always an inverse. That inverse may be expressed in three different ways. The most common is like `sin^-1(theta)`, which can be confusing because it does not mean `sin(1/theta)`, although `sin^2(theta)=(sin(theta))^2`. The biggest reason people can still understand the difference between `1/sin(theta)` and `sin^-1(theta)` is that each trigonometric function has a multiplicative inverse like `1/sin(theta)=csc(theta)`, so most people will use that inverse specifically. There are more less confusing ways to express other inverse however, like `arcsin(theta)`, which mean the same as `sin^-1(theta)`, but mathematicians decided to add the 'arc' prefix to make it less confusing.

Law of Sines

Law of Sines

On a triangle, each angle represents the proportion of a side to its adjacent sides. That side opposes the respective angle, and they will both have similar names, like angle 'B' and side 'b'. The law of sines describes how these proportions work: `a/sin(A)=b/sin(B)=c/sin(C)`, and can be converted to a reciprocal: `sin(A)/a=sin(B)/b=sin(C)/c`. To apply this to a real triangle, let's say we have `a=20`, `b=24`, and `B=30^@`. If we plug this into the equation, we get `sin(A)/20=sin(30)/24`, which can be simplified into `A=arcsin((20sin(30))/24)`. Therefore, we can get any side of a triangle if we have another side/angle pair and the angle opposite to the side we are calculating, or we can do the same with an angle.

Law of Cosines

Law of Cosines

This is another law used for solving any triangle when given three inputs. The law of cosines in its raw form is `c^2=a^2+b^2-2abcos(theta)`, but there is a different form for each of three different applications.

The third side of a triangle if two sides and the angle between them is known: `c=sqrt(a^2+b^2-2abcos(theta))`
The angles of a triangle if all three sides are known: `theta=arccos((a^2+b^2-c^2)/(2ab))`
The third side of a triangle if two sides and the angle opposite one of them is known: `a=bcos(theta)+-sqrt(c^2-b^2sin^2(theta))`
Finding Arc Length and Area of a Sector

Finding Arc Length and Area of a Sector

This topic is even easier than the previous ones. A circle looks exactly the same no matter how much it is rotated around its center, so we can use this to calculate portions of a circle. Let's start with an arc: what is the length of an arc with a radius of `5` and a subtended angle (angle of arc) of `33^@`:

First we calculate the circumference: `c=2rpi=>10pi`
Then calculate the portion of the circumference that arc inhabits: `p=33^@/360^@`
Then get the portion of the circumference the arc inhabits: `s=c*p=>10pi11/120=>11/12pi`

Next, let's look at solving for the area of a sector, or basically a piece of the circular pie. We'll use the same parameters for this example:

First we calulate the area (instead of the circumference): `a=r^2pi=>25pi`
Then calculate the portion of the area that the sector inhabits: `p=33^@/360^@`
Then get the portion of the area the sector inhabits: `s=a*p=>25pi11/120=>55/15pi`
Angles in Standard Position

Angles in Standard Position

An angle is the measure of the rotational difference between two intersecting lines. When using angles to reference a point on the circumference of a circle, we can easily draw a line from that point to the center of that circle, but where is the other line? This other line is drawn from the center of the circle in the direction of positive `x`. Definition of Imaginary and Complex Numbers

Definition of Imaginary and Complex Numbers

Many mathematicians realized that `sqrt4=2` and started asking more questions, like what is `sqrt(-1)`? Well, `1*1=1`, and `(-1)*(-1)=1`, so which number squares to `-1`? A greek mathematician named Heron of Alexandria was the first documented to have discovered the answer to this problem: the imaginary number `i`. Although this concept was not widely accepted until Leohnard Euler and Carl Freidrich Gauss' work, `i` is basically a unit to define numbers in another dimension perpendicular to the real number line. So `2-sqrt(-4)=2-2i`.

With these imaginary numbers, we can create what are called complex numbers. A complex number is a number with a real part and an imaginary part, like `2-2i`. All arithmetic acts the same with complex numbers as something like `2-2x`, just that `i^2=1`, so `(2-2i)^2=4-8i+4i^2=4-8i-4=8i`. This also means that `i^3=-i` and `i^4=1`. Now, we can actually graph these complex numbers just the same we would do with real numbers, just using another dimension. This plane is called the complex number plane, as opposed to the real number line. Here is an example of `-6+4i` on the complex number plane:

Complex Conjugates

Complex Conjugates

A complex conjugate is a complex number with an inverted imaginary part. For example, the complex conjugate of `4-i` is `4+i`, and vice versa. Complex conjugates can be very useful in solving complex equations, because multiplying a complex number by its conjugate will result in a real number: `(a+bi)(a-bi)=a^2+b^2`. Let's say we are solving for `x` in the equation `x^2+4=0`, we could solve this in two ways.

We could simply isolate x: `x^2=-4`
And then simplify: `x=+-2i`
Or, we could use complex conjugates to get the imaginary parts: `(x+2i)(x-2i)=0`
And grab the roots from that: `[-2i,2i]`

Polynomials

Polynomials

With any polynomial of degree `n`, we can easily determine its 'end' behaviors and get a list of how many bumps/curves it may have. For determining end behaviors, the `+x` end will tend towards `oo` inherently, if `n` is even the `-x` end tend towards `+oo` whereas if it is odd the `-x` end will tend towards `-oo`, and a negative coefficient for the largest polynomial will vertically invert both ends. The number of bumps or turns `b` is a range of integers where `0<=b< n` that follows the equation `(n-b)mod2=1`, or basically `b` is at intervals of two starting at `(n+1)mod2` and ending at `n-1`. This means that the equation `-2x^3+15x^2-4x+10` tends towards `+oo` in the `-x` direction and `-oo` in the `+x` direction, and has `[0,2]` bumps.

We can also gather the possible number of complex and imaginary zeroes, and even determine all potential rational zeroes. The total number of roots for any given polynomial will not exceed that polynomial's degree `n`. Any root can end up being complex, so the maximum number of complex zeroes is `n`. However, imaginary zeroes have to come in pairs, so the maximum number of imaginary zeroes is equal to `n-nmod2`. As for the possible real zeroes, with the polynomial in the form `ax^n+bx^(n-1)...k`, you can easily just divide the factors of `a` by the factors of `k`. So the possible real zeroes for `-2x^3+15x^2-4x+10` would be `+-1/1, +-1/2, +-1/5, +-1/10, +-2/1, +-2/2, +-2/5, +-2/10`, or simplified, `+-2, +-1, +-1/2, +-2/5, +-1/5, +-1/10`. Now, only up to three of these are actually roots for the given polynomial, but finding the possible zeroes will let you use a calculator to "plug n' play," which may be much easier.

For the graph of any polynomial, there may be local minimums and maximums. These are where the slope of the line crosses between negative and positive. Between each of these local limits, there will be an arc, and we can classify that arc interval in two different ways: concave up and concave down. To help determine which direction an interval is concave, think of a satellite dish. It is a concave mirror, and points towards where ever the respective satellite is. In the case of polynomial intervals, if the 'dish' is pointing down, it is concave down, and if it is pointing up it is respectively concave up. It will make a bit more sense why you should know this when you get into derivatives in calculus.

Rational Equations

Rational Equations

A rational equation is simply any equation with any sort of division in it. Solving rational equations is not really any different than normal equations, the only differences being rational asymptotes, and excluded values and extraneous solutions. An example of a function that has both a vertical and horizontal asymptote would be simply `f(x)=1/x`, because `1/0` is undefined and `1/x=0` is unsolvable. This also means that `x=0` would be an excluded value, because the equation cannot be solved when `x=0`. Now let's say that we have to solve `(4x)/(x-1)=4/(x-1)`. Simplifying it we would eventually get `x=1`, but evaluating our equation with this solution proves impossible because we cannot divide by zero, therefore the solution `x=1` is extraneous and the expression has no solutions.

To help us get asymptotes, we can try finding all situations we divide by zero, but this does not always yield all possible asymptotes or true ones, and this only works for vertical asymptotes. The best way to get all asymptotes is to graph your function and look at every vertical and horizontal line it nears but never reaches. The function has to tend towards `+oo` or `-oo` just before and after every asymptote to qualify it being an asymptote.

Definition of a Conic Section

A conic section is the curve where a cone and plane meet. They can come in four different shapes: circles, ellipses, hyperbolas, and parabolas. A circle forms when the plane is level, an ellipse when the plane's angle is less than that of the cone, a parabola when both angles are equal, and a hyperbola when the plane's angle is greater. Standard and General Form

Every different form of a conic section has their own simplified standard and general forms. You will notice that the variable `B` is missing in the general forms, only because it 'slants' the graph and is only useful in calculus.

Graphing Conic Sections

Graphing Conic Sections

Every conic section has their own limitations. Each one has at least one focus, and the parabola includes a directrix (focus as a line). With these values and the radii or limits, we can graph every single conic section. The center for every conic section is the same: `(h, k)`.

  • Circle

    All of the points on a circle must be the same distance (`r`) from the center. This means that the focus and the center are the same point. The circle is also the easiest to graph.

  • Ellipse

    An ellipse is similar to a circle, but of course, stretched in one dimension (or squashed in the other). The foci of an ellipse are along the major (larger) diameter, and assuming it is `a`, will be at `(+-sqrt(a^2-b^2),k)`. Also, the outer limits of an ellipse along `x` and `y` are `(h+-a,k)` and `(h,k+-b)`. Now that these important points are specified, the full definition of an ellipse states that for every point along the edge, the summed distances to foci must equal the major diameter (`2a`). This simplifies to an average of `a`, which a circle satisfies as well, and the circle is basically the square of ellipses.

  • Parabola

    Parabolas are a bit different from anything else because they have Directrices. In essence, every point on a parabola must be the same distance from the focus and vertically to the directrix, if the parabola is vertical. A vertical parabola is solved for `y`, while a horizontal parabola is solved for `x`. As usual, the center or vertex for a parabola is `(h,k)`, but the focus and directrix are a bit different. I am going to create a couple new variables here, `c` and `d`, where `a=1/(2(c-d))` and `k=(c+d)/2`. Simplified, `c=(4ak+1)/4a` and `d=(4ak-1)/4a`. This makes it way easier to find the focus and directrix, because the focus is at `(h,c)` and the directrix is at `y=d`. This also means that the focus and directrix are equidistant vertically to the vertex.

  • Hyperbola

    Hyperbolas are by far the most interesting conic sections, because they are basically ellipses inverted in a dimension. A hyperbola will have the two asymptotes `+- b/a(x-h)+k`, meaning the hyperbola will never intersect these lines. Hyperbolas also have foci and limits. In this case, I will be using a 'horizontal' hyperbola, so there will be a spot with no values along the `y` axis. For 'vertical' hyperbolas, just switch `x` and `y`, and `a` and `b` in my equations. When a hyperbola is simplified into standard form, if the right side is positive, it is a 'horizontal' hyperbola, and if the right side is negative, the hyperbola is respectively 'vertical'.

    The inner limits of a hyperbola are at `(h+-a,k)`, meaning that if `k=0` these points are the x-intercepts. The foci of a hyperbola are at `(+-sqrt(a^2+b^2),k)`, with only an operation change differentiating it from ellipses. Now, the absolute difference between the distance between every point on the hyperbola and both foci must be equal to `2a`. This is exactly like the ellipse, but again, one of the operations has switched.
