Point-Slope Form for a Line

Point-Slope Form for a Line

The equation for a line can come in many forms, and, if you want to be lazy, you can represent it in Point-Slope Form. Point-Slope Form is basically the median equation of Slope and Slope-Intercept Form. The actual form is `y-y_1=m(x-x_1)`. This represents the fact that the difference in two points' y values is proportional to the diference in their x values by a multiplier m. This form could easily be converted into the equation to find slope by dividing both sides by `x-x_1`, making it `m=(y-y_1)/(x-x_1)`, or it can be converted to Slope Intercept form by distributing `m` and adding both sides by `y`.

For an example, let's say we were given the slope: `3/2` and a point on the line: `(6,5)` and have to find the Slope-Intercept Form of that line:
First, we fill in the information we already know: `y-5=(3/2)(x-6)`
Then, we distribute `m` or the slope: `y-5=(3/2)x-9`
And add `y_1` to both sides: `y-5+5=(3/2)x-9+5`
And out comes our answer: `y=(3/2)x-4`
Now let's say that we have two points on a line: `(7,4)` and `(3,3)` and had to find the slope of that line:
First, we fill in the information we already know: `4-3=m(7-3)`
Then, we divide both sides by `(x-x_1)` to get the Slope Equation: `m(7-3)/(7-3)=(4-3)/(7-3)`
Simplify it: `m=(4-3)/(7-3)`
And out comes our answer: `m=1/4`

Where would this be used? This sort of math pops up in programs everywhere, including your Operating System (Windows/Mac OSX/Linux), your browser, and pretty much every single video game you have ever played. To use video games as an example, a 2D platformer with a ray-gun would need to calculate the slope of the line between your avatar and your cursor to show the beam. But, let's say you're not interested in computers. Well, if you do any sort of construction you pretty much need to use this, and if do any sort of statistics, it will certainly pop up.

Laws of Exponents and Simplifying Expressions with Exponents

Laws of Exponents and Simplifying Expressions with Exponents

There is a set of laws that apply to exponents and make it possible for us to solve expressions that have exponents. The laws are as follows, so don't let the number of `x`'s and `y`'s overwhelm you:

Law Example Explanation
`x^y` where `y>0` is equal to `1` multiplied to `x` `y` times `5^3=(1)*5*5*5` This is the basic purpose of exponents, to add another level to multiplication the same way multiplication did to addition.
`x^0=1` `1000^0=1` When there is nothing to multiply `1` to, `1` simply remains. Or, `1/1=1` and `x/x=1`.
`x^y` where `y<0` is equal to `1` divided by `x` `y` times `5^-3=1/(5*5*5)` This is still a purpose of exponents, just applied to negative numbers.
With the three above laws, when the exponent increases by 1, the value multiplies by x, when the exponent decreases by 1, the value divides by x.
`1/x^y=x^-y` `1/x^3=x^-3` `1/((x*x*x))=(1/(x*x*x))` or `1/((1/(x*x*x))) = (x*x*x)`.
`x^(1/y)=rooty x` `x^(1/3)=root3 x` `1/x` is the inverse of x, so in an exponent it results in the inverse: a root.
`x^y*x^z=x^(y+z)` `x^3*x^1=x x x*x=x^4` This is has the same type of reason that `x*y+x*z=x*(y+z)`, that we multiply `x` `y` times, and then `x` another `z` times, for a total of `y+z` times.
`x^y/x^z=x^(y-z)` `x^3/x^4=(x x x)/(x x x x)=1/x=x^-1` This expands out to `x^y+1/x^z`, which simplifies to `x^y+x^-z` and then to `x^(y-z)`.
`(x^y)^z=x^(y*z)` `(x^2)^2=(x x)^2=x x x x=x^4` First we multiply `x` `y` times, and then that another `z` times, for a total of `y*z`.
`(xy)^z=x^z*y^z` `(xy)^4=x x x xyyyy=x^4+y^4` Quite simply, we are distributing the exponent to each component of a multiplication expression because in the end it is all multiplied back together.
`(x/y)^z=x^z/y^z` `(x/y)^4=(x x x x)/(yyyy)=x^4/y^4` The same as previously, except some components are divided, but it is at the same level of operation because multiplication and division oppose directly.
`x^(y/z)=rootz x^y` `x^(3/4)=(x^3)^(1/4)=root4 (x^3)` This results from a conjugation of previous rules, expanding out to the root of `x^y` in the base of `z`.
We can use these laws to simplify exponential expressions, so let's simplify this one: `((x^4*x^-7)/x^5)^(1/5)`
First we evaluate the expression inside the parentheses: `x^4*x^-7=x^-3=>(x^-3/x^5)^(1/5)`
And continue: `x^-3/x^5=x^-8=>(x^-8)^(1/5)`
And use the last rule to get our answer: `(x^-8)^(1/5)=root5 (1/x^8)`

So in just three steps we were able to turn a daunting expression like `((x^4*x^-7)/x^5)^(1/5)` into simply `root5 (1/x^8)`, which is about as complex as a simplified exponential expression can get, because of these exponential laws. This is why it is being taught and taught again, so that everyone remembers it, because you might need to use it again.

Where would this be used? Admittedly, there are not as many reasons to use exponents in life, but they are definitely more important. If you have a savings account, the equation determining your profit in investment, and, if you have a loan, the equation determining your periodic fee, both use exponents. Now that example is a bit cliche, as everyone uses it because it is the most thought-of case, but an even more important application exists inside our brains already. The way our brains process sensory input is using logarithms, the other inverse to exponents, in the form of logarithmic scales. For example, have you ever noticed that the difference between two loud sounds seems to be less than the difference between two quieter sounds? I encourage you to try it on your device, just not too loud!

Composition of Functions

Composition of Functions

Composition of functions is compiling all unreferenced functions with their referenced functions, so there are no more function references in the end. For example, let's say we have two functions: `f(x)=10+3g(x)` and `g(x)=40x`. If we were to compose these two functions together, we would use `f(x)` as our base function and fill in any references within that. So, `3g(x)` would turn into `3(40x)`, making `f(x)=10+3(40x)`, or simplified: `f(x)=120x+10`.

Now, for a more complicated problem, let's say we have these six functions:
`a(x)=d(x)+4-30x`
`b(x)=2a(x)d(x)-10`
`c(x)=b(x)-x*a(x)`
`d(x)=47x`
`e(x)=c(x)-50`
`f(x)=(x-4)*e(x)`
The first thing we would do is map all of the references out creating six trees of functions:

In the example above, each red line shows where that function is referenced, meaning it cannot be the base function, and the only function without a red line is `f(x)`, meaning everything is going to be compiled into that function. If `f(x)` referenced another function other than `e(x)`, both `f(x)` and `e(x)` would be base functions, meaning you would end up with two functions instead of just one in the end. But, since we will end up with just `f(x)`, we need to look at every endpoint in the tree and compile upwards like so:

Step Result
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)->a(x)->d(x)` `d(x)=47x`
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)->a(x)` `(47x)+4-30x=>`
`a(x)=17x+4`
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)->b(x)` `2(17x+4)(47x)-10=>`
`b(x)=1598x^2+376x-10`
`f(x)->e(x)->c(x)` `(1598x^2+376x-10)-x*(17x+4)=>`
`c(x)=1581x^2+372x-10`
`f(x)->e(x)` `(1581x^2+372x-10)-50=>`
`e(x)=1581x^2+372x-60`
`f(x)` `(x-4)*(1581x^2+372x-60)=>`
`f(x)=1581x^3-5952x^2-1548x+240`

Believe it or not, you use Function Composition for almost every math problem you solve or every expression you create. To solve a problem, you need to follow the order of operations, and by doing so, you are following a tree up. For example, anything inside parentheses is basically a function of its own, it's just not named. When you are writing an expression however, you are using bits and pieces in your mathematical vocabulary to put it together like a puzzle piece, and each one of those pieces is a type of function. As for career uses, it is used all the time in computers, just not always mathematically. Function Composition can be compiling any set of functions, including functions with sets of computer instructions. Computer programs are written with functions, but they can be recursive, that is, call themselves multiple times, so the computer has to follow its function tree from the top down, but in essence still does the same thing. Sometimes, programmers will do this too, because when programs have unnessecary functions, the program gets a bit slower and larger, so for optimization, the programmer might do some of the computer's work for it.

Finding Inverse Functions

Finding Inverse Functions

When you graph the inverse of a function, something special happens: that function is reflected across the line `y=x`. To get the inverse of a function, you need to solve for the other, or another, variable. In the case of functions for graphing, we will also switch the names of `x` and `y`. So let's say we had the function `y=3x+4`, to get the inverse we would need to solve `x=3y+4` for `y`.

For a more complex example, how about we invert `y=7*4^3-5^x`. Table time!
Get the actual equation we are solving for: `7*4^3-5^y=x`
Simplify: `448-5^y=x`
Subtract by `x` and `-5^y` `5^y=448-x`
Use logarithms to isolate `y`, and we get our answer: `y=log_5(448-x)`

This concept can actually be quite useful when trying to test the input variables for a certain output. To use the above equation, let's say if we put in the number of members of a group we would get out a certain number of dollars, and if we wanted to get enough money to pay rent or whatever, we could invert the function and get how many members we would need. You might use this for planning how many people you can buy food for in a party, but most other examples are in fully mathematical applications, usually mathematical modeling and statistics.

Simplifying Square Roots and Rationalizing the Denominator

Simplifying Square (and other) Roots and Rationalizing the Denominator

When simplifying an expression, any roots of any base must be removed as much as possible, and the denominator of every fraction must not contain any roots. To simplify a root, the contents of that root must have a multiple that is of that root's base. To clarify, `rootx ((y^x)z)` can be simplified to `y*rootx z`. For a fraction, to remove any radicals, the entire fraction must be multiplied by a factor that will remove the radicals in the denominator, most likely making the numerator irrational in the process.

Let's simplify `5/root3(54x)`:
First, we have to simplify the radical: `54=3^3*2->5/(3root3(2x))`
Then remove the radical from the denominator: `(5root3(2x)^2)/6x`
And simplify: `(5root3(4x^2))/6x`

Easy, only three steps! This however is another one of those topics that doesn't get much use except in school, unless you are a carpenter, physicist, or pretty much anyone dealing with square roots (ie. pythagorean theorem) and fractions. Although, it is still not a bad thing to learn, because it is still used all over the place. You might need it someday!

Factoring Quadratics and some Cubics

Factoring Quadratics and some Cubics

To quickly review, a quadratic is an expression in the form of `ax^2+bx+c`, but factoring them is really quite easy. There are two ways to do it: use the 'generic' method, or use the quadratic formula. The generic method works for simple cases, and if it doesn't work for your case you can use the quadratic formula instead. The steps for the generic method are laid out for you below, and `f(x,y)` is the common factor of `x` and `y`:

Step Example
Factor out all common factors `zax^2+zbx+zc=z*(ax^2+bx+c)`
Find which two numbers multiply to `ac` and add to `b` `st=ac and s+t=b->z*(ax^2+(s+t)x+c)`
Factor the resultant numbers in an array `z*(f(a,s)x+f(t,c))(f(a,t)x+f(s,c))`

This method is a bit confusing and hard to put into a formula, but if you have paper and do not have a calulator, this would be the easiest way to go. With the quadratic formula, you will want a calculator, especially since it uses roots and fractions. Below is an example of using the quadratic formula to solve a quadratic expression.

The Quadratic Formula: `(b+-sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a)`
Putting it into your expression: `a(x-(b+sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))(x-(b-sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))`
To complete the factoring, you will need to distribute factors of `a` to each parenthetic group intuitively so you get the best answer possible: `a=ts->(tx-t(b+sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))(sx-s(b-sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a))`

For a cubic or any other higher polynomial, you will need to use the Rational Root Theorem, which explains that all rational roots must be in `+-(f(c_(x^n)))/(f(c_(x^0))` where `f(x)` returns all factors of `x`, `n` is the highest polynomial, `c` is the coefficient of its base, and `c_(x^n)!=0` and `c_(x^0)!=0`. So, if we have `3x^4-2`, all rational roots must fit in `+-(1,3)/(1,2)`. To find all of these roots, you can only repeat testing the expression to find inputs/roots that result in `0`, appending the root as a "parenthetic group" in the form of `(x-r)` where r is your root, and dividing the original expression by this group creating the next 'original' expression. Then, once you can't find any more roots, multiply your output by your last 'original' group, and finally intuitively distribute factors of `a` to each parenthetic group you created. Usually only mathematicians and statisticians actually use quadratics and cubics, but you will still need to know this to pass Unit 6, which will obviously come later on, so don't forget this!

Direct and Inverse Variations

Direct and Inverse Variations

When two variables have a relationship where both variables scale similarly, there are two ways to describe it: direct and inverse. A direct relationship between two variables would follow the equation `y=kx` where `k` is a constant, so that when `y` is multiplied by `a`, `x` scales by `a` as well. An inverse relationship however, would follow `y=k/x`, meaning that when `y` is multiplied by `a`, `x` scales by `1/a`, or in other words, scales inversely. However, a simplified expression that contains any constant or variable that is added or is an exponent cannot be a direct or inverse relationship, because both variables do not scale exactly inverse or the same. For example, neither `y=x+1`, `y=x^2`, or `y=x*z` can have a direct or inverse variation, unless `z` is a constant in the last example.
